How to Separate Your Personal and Business Finances in Cameroon

Mixing personal and business money is the most common financial mistake small business owners in Cameroon make. It costs them more than they realise — and it is entirely preventable.

Ask most small business owners in Cameroon where their business money is, and the honest answer is: mixed with everything else. The same mobile money account handles business sales and school fees. The same cash in the till pays for groceries and supplier invoices. When business is good, personal spending goes up. When business is slow, it's hard to tell if the problem is sales or personal expenses.

This is the single most common financial mistake in Cameroonian small businesses. And it is almost entirely invisible until the damage is already done.

Why mixing finances is so costly

When personal and business money are combined, three things happen consistently. First, you can't know your real profit — because some of what looks like "revenue" has already been spent on personal items, and some of what looks like "expenses" are actually personal costs. Second, your business appears less profitable than it is on paper — because personal spending inflates the apparent cost base. Third, you can't produce a financial statement that a bank will accept, because there is no clean boundary between the business and you personally.

The mental accounting trap

Most business owners who mix finances believe they can track it in their head. "I know which money is for the business and which is personal." This works for a week. It fails entirely over a month, because the volume of small transactions — 500 XAF here, 2,000 XAF there — accumulates into an unrecoverable blur. By the time you try to reconcile at the end of the month, the mental accounting is gone and the actual accounting was never done.

Step 1 — Create a separate Mobile Money account for your business

The most practical first step in Cameroon is dedicating a separate Mobile Money number to your business. All business payments come in on this number. All business expenses go out from this number. Your personal Mobile Money handles everything personal. This single separation — which costs nothing — immediately creates a visible boundary between your business cash flow and your personal finances.

Step 2 — Pay yourself a fixed amount

The most common way personal and business finances blur is through irregular withdrawals. You take 5,000 XAF for transport. Then 20,000 for groceries. Then another 10,000 for something else. None of these are recorded as expenses, none are consistent, and over a month they can represent a significant portion of your revenue with no documentation.

The solution is to pay yourself a fixed monthly or weekly amount — treat it as a salary from your business to yourself. This amount goes from the business account to your personal account on a fixed schedule. Everything else stays in the business. This creates clarity on both sides: you know exactly what you're drawing from the business, and the business knows exactly what it's spending on your salary.

Step 3 — Record every expense in a category

Every amount that leaves your business — rent, stock purchases, transport, utilities, staff salaries, your own salary — needs to be recorded as an expense with a category. This serves two purposes. First, it shows you where the money actually goes, which almost always surprises business owners when they see it for the first time. Second, it builds the expense history that banks require for loan applications.

What separation makes possible

When personal and business finances are cleanly separated, three things become possible that weren't before. First, you can have an honest conversation with a bank or investor about your business — because the numbers are real and verifiable. Second, you can make better decisions about when to hire, expand, or invest — because you know the actual financial position of the business. Third, you stop feeling the constant low-level anxiety of not knowing whether the business is making or losing money.
