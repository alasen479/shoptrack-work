How to Price Your Products to Make a Real Profit

Most small business owners in Cameroon price by feel — 'what the market charges'. But without calculating your real cost, you can be busy every day and still lose money.

Walk through any market in Douala or Yaoundé and you'll find business owners who are working hard, serving customers all day, and yet struggling to have money at the end of the month. The problem is rarely effort. It's almost always pricing.

Most small businesses in Cameroon price their products one of two ways: they copy what competitors charge, or they guess based on what feels right. Both methods can destroy your profit without you ever realising it.

The real cost of a product

Every item you sell has a true cost. Not just what you paid for it — but everything it took to get it into your shop and into a customer's hands. This includes:

  • Purchase price — what you paid your supplier
  • Transport — if you travel to Nigeria, Lagos, or Douala to buy goods, your transport cost must be spread across every item you bought
  • Import duties and fees — any taxes or border charges
  • Storage losses — items that expire, get damaged, or go unsold
  • Your time — the hours you spend buying, transporting, and stocking the goods

Most business owners only count the purchase price. This is why they think they're making a profit when they're actually breaking even — or worse.

The simple formula you need

Your minimum selling price is your total cost per item multiplied by your desired margin. A simple but effective formula for small retail businesses:

Selling price = Total cost per unit × (1 + target margin)

For example: you buy a dress for 8,000 XAF and spend 1,500 XAF in transport costs per item. Your total cost is 9,500 XAF. If you want a 40% margin, your minimum selling price is 9,500 × 1.4 = 13,300 XAF. Anything below this and you're not making a real profit.

Why most businesses don't know their margin

Knowing your margin requires two things: knowing your exact cost price, and knowing your actual selling price. Most businesses handle this with a notebook or memory — which means the numbers are either wrong or unknown.

The competitor pricing trap

When business owners say "I sell at the market price", they're assuming every competitor has the same costs. But if your competitor bought from a cheaper supplier, has a warehouse closer to the market, or sells in much higher volume, their cost structure is completely different from yours.

Matching a competitor's price without matching their costs is a formula for slow financial damage.

Three pricing strategies that work in Cameroon

1. Cost-plus pricing

Calculate your total cost per item, then add your target margin. Simple, reliable, and ensures you never sell at a loss. The challenge is knowing your total cost accurately — which requires recording every expense properly.

2. Value-based pricing

Price based on what the item is worth to the customer, not what it cost you. This works well for products with strong demand, limited competition, or high perceived value. A branded item, a specialty product, or something hard to find locally can often carry a much higher margin than a generic equivalent.

3. Bundle pricing

Group items together and price the bundle at a slight discount compared to buying individually. The customer feels they're getting a deal; you sell more volume and move slower items. Common in fashion boutiques — "buy this dress and get a matching bag at half price."

What happens when you get pricing right

When your prices are based on real costs, three things happen. First, you stop having months where you worked hard and have nothing to show for it. Second, you can start confidently giving customers discounts when needed — because you know exactly where your floor is. Third, your financial reports start making sense, which means your bank loan application tells a compelling story.
