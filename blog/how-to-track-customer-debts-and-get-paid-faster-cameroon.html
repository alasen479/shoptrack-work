How to Track Customer Debts and Get Paid Faster in Cameroon

Selling on credit is unavoidable in Cameroon. But untracked credit destroys cash flow silently. Here is how to manage customer debts professionally — and collect faster.

Walk into almost any small business in Cameroon and you will find a notebook with a page labelled "dettes" or "credit." Names, amounts, dates — scrawled in pen, sometimes crossed out, sometimes not. This system works until it doesn't. And when it breaks down, it breaks down expensively.

Credit sales are not the problem. The problem is untracked credit — debts that are forgotten, disputed, or simply never followed up on because the process of chasing them is uncomfortable and time-consuming.

Why credit goes unpaid in Cameroon

There are three main reasons customers don't pay their debts. The first is genuine forgetfulness — they meant to pay and simply lost track of it. The second is strategic delay — they know you'll feel awkward chasing them personally, so they wait until you bring it up. The third is the absence of a formal record — if there's nothing written down that both parties acknowledge, the debt becomes a matter of memory versus memory, which rarely resolves cleanly.

The solution to all three is the same: a professional, systematic tracking and reminder process that removes the personal awkwardness from the equation entirely.

The real cost of untracked debt

If your business extends credit to 20 customers per month at an average of 15,000 XAF each, that's 300,000 XAF in outstanding credit at any given time. If 30% of that goes uncollected or significantly delayed — which is common without a tracking system — you are absorbing 90,000 XAF per month in cash flow gaps. Over a year, that is more than 1,000,000 XAF that passed through your business but never landed in your pocket.

Step 1 — Record every credit sale at the point of sale

The moment a customer takes goods on credit, it must be recorded immediately. Not later that evening. Not when you remember. At the moment of the transaction. This means the customer's name, the amount, the items, the date, and ideally an agreed repayment date.

In ShopTrack, when you record a sale you choose the payment method — cash, Mobile Money, or credit. If the customer pays partially, you record the partial payment and ShopTrack automatically calculates the outstanding balance. The debt is immediately visible on your dashboard.

Step 2 — Send a professional WhatsApp confirmation

The moment a credit sale is recorded, ShopTrack sends the customer a WhatsApp message showing their name, the items purchased, the total amount, any partial payment made, and the outstanding balance. This single step changes the dynamic completely. The customer has a written record they acknowledged. The debt is no longer a verbal agreement — it's a documented transaction.

Most customers who receive this confirmation pay faster — not because they were going to avoid paying, but because it signals that you have a system. Systems command more respect than notebooks.

Step 3 — Follow up with automatic reminders

ShopTrack allows you to send a WhatsApp payment reminder to any customer in one tap. The message is professional and formal — it shows the customer's name, the original transaction, the outstanding amount, and your payment details. It is not a personal message from you. It is a business communication from ShopTrack on behalf of your business.

This distinction matters enormously in Cameroon where many credit relationships are personal. When the reminder comes from a system rather than from you personally, customers are far less likely to feel singled out and far more likely to pay promptly.

Step 4 — Know who owes you at all times

Your ShopTrack dashboard shows your complete outstanding debt position in real time — total amount owed, broken down by customer, with the date each debt was incurred. You can see at a glance who has been owing for three days versus three weeks. This information lets you prioritise which customers to follow up with first and identify patterns — customers who consistently pay late, customers who never need reminders, customers whose credit limit should be reduced.

What changes when you have a system

Business owners who implement a formal debt tracking system consistently report three changes within the first 60 days. First, the average time to payment drops significantly — customers pay within days rather than weeks when they know a record exists. Second, the total outstanding debt decreases — simply because more debts are being tracked and followed up on. Third, the awkward conversations disappear — because the process is handled by the system, not by you personally.
