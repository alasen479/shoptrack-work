How to Know If Your Business Is Actually Making Money

Many business owners in Cameroon confuse being busy with being profitable. You can serve customers all day and still lose money. Here is how to know the difference.

There is a question that every small business owner in Cameroon should be able to answer in 30 seconds: is my business actually making money? Not revenue. Not how much passed through the till. The actual profit — what's left after every expense is paid.

Most business owners cannot answer this question accurately. They have a feeling — "this was a good month" or "things are slow" — but not a number. And running a business on feelings rather than numbers is one of the main reasons businesses that seem busy still struggle financially.

Revenue and profit are completely different numbers

Revenue is every franc that comes in. Profit is what's left after everything that goes out. The gap between the two — all your costs — determines whether your business is actually sustainable.

A business that does 500,000 XAF in monthly sales sounds successful. But if the cost of goods is 300,000 XAF, rent is 80,000 XAF, transport is 30,000 XAF, utilities are 15,000 XAF, and staff costs are 50,000 XAF — total costs are 475,000 XAF. Profit: 25,000 XAF. That is 5% margin on 500,000 XAF of activity. One bad month, one large unexpected expense, and the business is in deficit.

The costs that most business owners miss

Cost of goods sold

This is the most important cost and the one most often calculated incorrectly. If you bought a dress for 8,000 XAF in Lagos and sold it for 15,000 XAF, your gross margin is 7,000 XAF — not 15,000 XAF. Every time you forget to count the purchase price as a cost, you overstate your profit.

Transport and sourcing costs

The trip to Douala, Lagos, or China to buy stock costs money. That cost belongs to the goods you bought on that trip. If you spent 50,000 XAF in transport and bought 100 items, each item costs 500 XAF more than its purchase price. Most business owners absorb this cost invisibly and wonder why margins feel thin.

Time and opportunity cost

Your time managing the shop, chasing debts, and handling administration is a cost. This doesn't appear on any statement, but it's real. A business that requires 60 hours a week of your time at margins too thin to pay yourself properly isn't profitable — it's a job with extra stress.

Slow-moving and dead stock

Stock that doesn't sell ties up cash. A dress sitting on the shelf for six months isn't just unsold — it represents cash you spent that hasn't returned, storage space you're using, and capital you can't deploy elsewhere. Dead stock should be counted as a cost.

How to calculate your real profit

The formula is simple: Revenue minus cost of goods sold minus operating expenses equals profit. The challenge is capturing all three numbers accurately and consistently.

What healthy margins look like in Cameroon retail

For fashion and clothing retail, healthy gross margins typically fall between 40% and 70%. For cosmetics and beauty products, 30% to 60%. For general merchandise, 25% to 50%. If your margins are consistently below these ranges, you either have a pricing problem, a cost problem, or both. Knowing your margin is the first step to fixing it.

The monthly check that changes everything

Once a month, run your profit and loss report. Compare this month to last month. Compare this quarter to the same quarter last year. Look at which product categories make you the most money. Look at which months are strong and which are weak. This 30-minute monthly review, done consistently, is worth more than any business advice you'll ever receive — because it's built on your actual numbers.
